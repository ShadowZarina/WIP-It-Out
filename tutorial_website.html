Lesson 1: Basic Shapes (Shapes and Forms) and Linework

In general, art is a subject that focuses on the creative aspect of your mind. As everyone says, “the only limit is your imagination”. However, being creative without knowing any basic fundamentals will cause you to struggle in creating art. This aspect tests your logical side, being able to ascertain how, and where to apply the basic fundamentals allows a person to think beyond the box in order to create their masterpiece.

Tutorial Videos

Every artist must learn basic lines and shapes as the fundamentals of art come from the ability to break down complex objects into simple shapes and lines.

The Elements of Art

Art as a medium utilizes varying elements and principles as their fundamentals, we will tackle each later one by one. So, let us begin first with line and shape.

Line
Defined as two points that connect to each other. Lines can either vary in weight (how bold the line is), curvature, and straightness, however so long as it connects two points, that is still considered a line.
Shape
When lines are connected to each other, they form a shape. A shape is a two-dimensional object or form that contains both width and height. Do not mistake shape with form, a form is a 3D object, having length, width, and height, whereas a 2D object has only width and height.
Types of Shapes
Geometric Shapes
Shapes that have a clear edge or boundary defined by mathematics. These include: squares, rectangles, and circles.
Organic Shapes
Shapes that do not have a defined edge or boundary, and are usually found in nature. Leaves, clouds, rivers, all of these are shapes that are free-form, flowy, and natural as they do not have a rigid form.

Learning to draw organic shapes allows you to create more expressive art, and allows you to utilize the principles as well. Well, let us learn a quick technique called “Breaking down forms.”

Form
Lines are connected together with an additional third dimension, length or depth. This extra dimension allows objects to occupy the third-dimension, and allows us to make these objects feel more three-dimensional even if the drawing itself is two-dimensional.

Applying forms is as simple as adding a third dimension to your drawings. This means turning your square into a cube, a rectangle into a box, etc. Try to practice drawing simple two dimensional shapes, then turning those into three dimensional forms.

In order to break down forms, find any three dimensional object you want to draw and try to simplify it into very simple shapes. For example, a plant pot can be simplified into organic shapes or forms for the leaves and geometric forms for the pot. Learning to “break down forms” is an essential skill especially when it comes to learning human anatomy later on.

Line weight allows us to emphasize which object should be the focus of everyone’s attention, or if an object is closer or farther from the viewer.

In conclusion:
  • Lines are two connected points
  • Shapes are connected lines, and are either geometric or organic.
  • Geometric shapes are more rigid defined edges that are based on mathematics.
  • Organic shapes are more free-form, not having clear edges that are based on nature.

Lesson 2: Value and Shading

Tutorial Videos

Value and shading allows shapes to feel more three-dimensional, building this feeling of depth through forms. You would have to draw lines on a circle that warp to give the illusion of depth, but with values and shading, you will be able to make that illusion more convincing.

Value
Defined as the degree of lightness and darkness of your image. The higher the value, the darker the image is; the lesser the value, the lighter the image is.
Shading
The smoothness or transition between your varying values of your image.
Lighting
Defined as how illumination hits an object. Strategic use of lighting allows for objects to gain more depth.
The Five Parts of Lighting
Highlight
The brightest reflected light source, most of the time containing no to less value at all (white).
Mid tone
The second brightest reflected light source containing less to some value.
Core Shadow
Area of the object where light is not being hit, contains the most value of the entire object.
Reflected Light
Area of the object where some light from the ground reflects back to the object.
Cast shadow
The shadow that is casted from the light to the ground due to being blocked by the object.

Lesson 3: Color Basics

Through this lesson, you will learn what makes up a color and the different types of color combinations!

Tutorial Videos

Color in an image brings out personality, style, and pleasure to the eyes. To understand color, we need to look into color theory, which stems from the “color wheel.”

Components of Color

Hue
Defined as the different wavelengths of color. In color theory, this is what we call “hues.”
Saturation
It is how intense, or “present” your color is. More saturation means your color is more visible and neon.
Tones
A hue that is desaturated (or gray).
Value
How bright or dark your color is.
Tints
A hue that is mixed with shades of white.
Shades
A hue that is mixed with shades of dark.
Ambient Light
All of the natural light that surrounds the object based on the environment.

Color Theory Concepts

Color temperature
Defined by artists as how “cool” (closer to blue) or “warm” (closer to red) a color is.
Complementary
A color combination where you take one hue and another that equally opposes it on the color wheel.
Analogous
A color combination where you utilize the surrounding hues on the color wheel based on your chosen hue.
Triadic
A color combination that utilizes evenly and widely placed hues forming a triangle on the wheel.
